💤
Sleep
  • No schedule yet — this is survival mode. Sleep happens in fragments around the clock and that's completely normal.
  • Cap any single nap at 2 hours during the day to help nudge night vs. day awareness. Keep nighttime feeds calm and dark.
  • Day/night confusion typically resolves by 6–8 weeks on its own. Bright days and quiet, dark nights will speed this along.
💡 Tip: You cannot create bad habits at this age. Hold, rock, feed to sleep — do whatever works. Foundation-building comes later.
🍼
Feeding
🕐
Frequency
Every 1.5–3 hrs around the clock (8–12 feeds/24 hrs). Stomachs are tiny — about the size of a marble at birth.
🌙
Night Feeds
Feed every 2–3 hrs overnight until back to birth weight. Do not let a newborn go more than 3–4 hrs without feeding.
📈
Growth Spurts
Growth spurts at 1–2 weeks and 3 weeks — expect cluster feeding and increased fussiness for 2–3 days.
✅
Good Signs
6+ wet diapers/day after Day 5, 3–4 dirty diapers/day, steady weight gain after Day 4.
🌱
Developmental Milestones
Motor
  • Holds head up briefly during tummy time
  • Moves arms and legs when awake
  • Opens hands briefly
  • Strong grasp reflex — grips your finger tightly
  • May begin turning head side to side when on tummy — reflex-driven at this age
Social & Emotional
  • Calms when picked up or spoken to
  • Looks at your face intently
  • Reacts to loud sounds
  • Beginning to distinguish familiar voices
Communication
  • Cries to communicate hunger, discomfort, tiredness
  • Makes sounds other than crying starting to emerge
  • Watches you as you move
Month 1 milestones are largely reflexive. Focus on feeding well, safe sleep, and skin-to-skin bonding. Every interaction is developmental.

Things to Know This Month
1
Tummy time starts day one
Begin tummy time from the first day home — even 1–2 minutes several times a day counts. It prevents flat head and builds the neck strength needed for every future milestone.
2
Talk, sing, and narrate constantly
Your voice is the most stimulating thing in his world right now. Narrate your day, sing anything, make eye contact. Language development starts here.
3
Safe sleep on every surface, every time
Back to sleep, firm flat surface, no soft bedding — every nap, every night, every caregiver. Room-share without bed-sharing per AAP guidelines.
4
The witching hour is real (and temporary)
Most babies have intense, inconsolable fussiness in the late afternoon/evening (5–11 PM) — called the Period of PURPLE Crying. It starts around weeks 2–3, peaks at 6 weeks, and typically resolves by 3–4 months. It's not colic, not hunger, not pain — it's a normal developmental phase. Soothe with: dim lights, loud white noise, swaddle, skin-to-skin, motion. If nothing works, put baby safely in the crib and take a 10-minute break. Never shake a baby.
5
1-month well visit
Weight check, length, head circumference, and physical exam. Your pediatrician will check healing (umbilical cord, circumcision if applicable), jaundice, feeding, and growth trajectory. A great time to voice any concerns. No vaccines at this visit — that comes at 2 months.

💤
Sleep
  • Day/night confusion is resolving. You may start seeing a slightly longer stretch at night — 3–5 hours is a win at this age.
  • Naps are still wildly unpredictable (20 min to 2 hrs). Don't try to schedule them yet — watch wake windows and sleepy cues instead.
  • Bedtime is still late (8–10 PM is normal). An earlier bedtime will come naturally around 3–4 months as the circadian rhythm matures.
  • Witching hour peaks this month — intense evening fussiness (5–11 PM) is at its worst around 6 weeks. This is the Period of PURPLE Crying: normal, developmental, and temporary. It resolves by 3–4 months. Dim lights, white noise, motion, and skin-to-skin are your best tools.
💡 Tip: Start a simple pre-sleep routine now — even just dim lights, a feed, and a song. Babies recognize patterns as early as 8 weeks.

🕐

Every 2–3 hrs during the day (8–12 feeds/24 hrs). Feedings may start to space out slightly as stomach capacity grows.
🌙
Night Feeds
1–3 night feeds still normal and expected. Some babies start a longer 4–5 hr stretch — a sign the circadian rhythm is developing.
📈
Growth Spurts
6-week growth spurt is significant — expect cluster feeding for 2–4 days. Very common around this time.

Developmental Milestones
Motor
  • Holds head up 45° during tummy time
  • Moves both arms and legs more symmetrically
  • Opens and shuts hands with more intention
  • Pushes up slightly when on tummy
  • Tracks moving objects with eyes (left to right)
Social & Emotional
  • Social smile — smiles back when you smile and talk to him
  • Calms at the sound of a familiar voice
  • Recognizes your face at close range
  • Shows excitement with arm and leg movements
Communication
  • Cooing sounds begin — soft vowel sounds (ooh, ahh)
  • Reacts to loud sounds by startling or looking
  • Makes sounds other than crying more frequently
The social smile is the big one this month — it's a true developmental milestone (CDC 2-month checklist). If you're not seeing it by 8 weeks, mention it at the next well visit.

Things to Know This Month
1
Tummy time every wake window
Aim for 15–20 min total tummy time per day across multiple short sessions. Use the Lovevery Ladybug or your face as motivation. He should be lifting his head noticeably more this month.
2
Talk and copy his sounds
When he coos, coo back. Mirror his sounds and facial expressions. This back-and-forth is how language develops — long before actual words.
3
2-month well visit
Includes first round of vaccines (DTaP, Hib, IPV, PCV, Rotavirus). Expect fussiness and low-grade fever for 24–48 hrs. Acetaminophen dosing per your pediatrician.
4
Respond to every cry
Still cannot self-soothe at this age. Responding quickly actually reduces crying over time — you're building secure attachment, not bad habits.
5
Watch for postpartum changes
6-week mark is when postpartum depression and anxiety often peak. Both parents can experience this. Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale is a standard screening tool — speak to your OB if anything feels off.

Sleep
  • Sleep is starting to consolidate. Some babies stretch to 4–6 hours overnight — follow hunger cues and don't wake a sleeping baby.
  • Naps are still unpredictable but should be capped at 2 hours each to protect night sleep and ensure daytime calories.
  • Bedtime is shifting earlier — many babies land between 7–9 PM depending on nap length and wake time. Watch for sleepy cues rather than chasing a fixed time.
💡 Heads up: The 4-month sleep regression is approaching. If sleep suddenly gets harder at the end of this month, that's likely why.

Every 2.5–4 hrs during the day (7–9 feeds/24 hrs). Feedings are becoming more efficient — sessions may get shorter but that's normal.

Things to Know This Month
1
Tummy time is non-negotiable
Aim for 15–30 min spread throughout the day (several short sessions). It builds neck, shoulder, and core strength needed for rolling, sitting, and crawling.
2
Talk constantly — it builds language
Narrate your day, repeat his sounds back, make eye contact when you talk. Every conversation now builds the language foundation he'll use for years.
3
Consistency is starting to matter
His circadian rhythm is beginning to develop. A consistent morning wake time and bedtime routine (even simple: bath, feed, dim lights, sleep) starts building healthy sleep habits now.
4
Swaddle transition is coming
Since he's showing rolling signs, swaddle must stop immediately. Arms-free sleep sack keeps him warm safely. AAP safe sleep: firm flat surface, on back, no loose items.
5
The 4-month sleep regression is coming
Around weeks 14–18, sleep architecture permanently changes. He may wake more, fight naps, and seem extra fussy. It's normal — not a problem to fix.

💤
Sleep
  • The 4-month regression is here. This is a permanent, biological shift to adult-like sleep cycles — not something you did wrong.
  • Babies now cycle through light sleep every 60–120 min and may fully wake between cycles. How they fall asleep at bedtime = how they'll need to fall back asleep overnight.
  • Naps may shorten to 20–30 min. Aim for a 7–8 PM bedtime — consistent routine, dark room, and sound machine are your best tools right now.
💡 Tip: Formal sleep training isn't recommended before 5 months, but practicing drowsy-but-awake at bedtime now builds the foundation.
🍼
Feeding
🕐
Frequency
Every 3–4 hrs during the day (6–8 feeds). Feedings are becoming more efficient — sessions may get shorter.
🌙
Night Feeds
1–2 night feeds still common. The 4-month regression may temporarily increase night waking — this is developmental, not hunger-driven.
📈
Growth Spurts
Growth spurt around 4 months — increased hunger for a few days. Feed on demand during this time.
✅
Good Signs
Weight gain slows slightly — about 0.5–1 oz/day now. Well visit at 4 months includes weight check.
🌱
Developmental Milestones
Motor
  • Pushes up onto elbows/forearms during tummy time
  • Reaches for and bats objects with intention
  • Brings hands together at midline
  • Pushes down on legs when feet are placed on a firm surface
  • May show early rolling attempts — some babies roll tummy to back this month, but 6 months is the benchmark
Social & Emotional
  • Smiles spontaneously to get your attention (CDC 4-month milestone)
  • Chuckles when you try to make him laugh
  • Looks at you and makes sounds to get attention
  • Recognizes familiar people from across the room
Communication
  • Makes sounds back when you talk — back-and-forth cooing conversations
  • Turns head toward the sound of your voice
  • Looks at hands with interest — discovering his own body
The CDC 4-month checklist benchmarks: spontaneous smiling to get attention, chuckling, making sounds back when you talk. These are things 75%+ of babies do by this age.

Tummy time every day

Sleep

Sleep
  • By 6 months, many babies are physiologically capable of sleeping 6+ hour stretches without feeding — but 25–50% still wake overnight, which is also normal.
  • Most babies are on 3 semi-predictable naps. The third nap (catnap) often gets shorter and harder to achieve — a sign the 3→2 nap transition is on the horizon.
  • 6 months is the most common time to begin sleep training if you choose to. AAP recommends placing baby drowsy-but-awake.
💡 Tip: Blackout curtains make a meaningful difference at this age. Light signals the brain to wake up — darkness protects naps and early mornings.
🍼
Feeding
🕐
Frequency
5–7 milk feeds/day plus 1–2 solid food introductions. Milk is still primary nutrition — solids are exploration at this stage.
🌙
Night Feeds
Many babies drop to 0–1 night feeds if sleep training has begun. Some still need 1 feed overnight — normal.
📈
Growth Spurts
Solids introduction brings changes in diaper output (less frequent, different consistency). This is normal.
✅
Good Signs
6+ wet diapers/day, consistent weight gain, seems satisfied after milk feeds. Baby is exploring food — swallowing some is enough at this stage. Milk is still primary nutrition.

Things to Know This Month
1
Start solids around 6 months
Look for readiness cues: sits with support, good head control, interest in food, tongue-thrust reflex fading. Start with single-ingredient foods — iron-rich foods first (pureed meat, iron-fortified oatmeal).
2
Introduce allergens early
AAP recommends introducing peanut butter, eggs, and other top allergens early and repeatedly — at least 3x/week. This significantly reduces allergy risk. Introduce one at a time and watch for reactions.
3
6-month well visit
Includes third vaccine series and developmental screening. Discuss solids plan, sleep, and any concerns.
4
Floor time over containers
He needs floor time to build strength for crawling. Limit bouncers, swings, and baby containers during wake windows.
5
Stranger anxiety is starting
Completely normal and a sign of healthy attachment. Prepare new caregivers that he may take longer to warm up.

Sleep
  • The 3→2 nap transition typically begins between 7–9 months. Signs: consistently fighting the third nap, or taking it very late and struggling at bedtime.
  • When dropping the third nap, move bedtime earlier (as early as 6:00–6:30 PM) to avoid overtiredness during the adjustment — usually takes 2–4 weeks.
  • Sleep challenges between 7–10 months are common — driven by new mobility, teething, and separation anxiety, not regression in skills.
💡 Tip: Give plenty of daytime practice for new skills (rolling, sitting, crawling). Babies who practice during the day are less likely to rehearse at night. If you haven't sleep trained yet, 7 months is still a great window — most methods work well between 5–9 months.

Sleep
  • Wake windows are lengthening to 3–4 hours. If naps are suddenly harder, try shifting them 15–30 min later before assuming a nap drop.

Things to Know This Month
1
Point to and name everything constantly

Don't rush walking
Cruising and pulling to stand is the right developmental stage. Walking ranges from 9–15 months (CDC updated benchmark).

AAP recommends no screen time except video calls until 18–24 months. Screens during meals or play are particularly disruptive to language development.
5
Start thinking about 12-month transition
At 12 months: whole cow's milk, dropping bottles, more table foods, and possibly starting to think about eventual 2-to-1 nap transition (though most babies stay on 2 until 14–18 months).
